The heat of a volcanic landscape is sometimes overrated because the elevation generally creates sharp transition to cold once the sun goes down.  The residents of hotshoe need a source of fuel for heating, cooking and their crafting.  Create two plausible sources of fuel and how the practical implication of how they connect the people with the landscape.

Heat from the volcano might be within reach of the flora ([[Ash yam]]) and related to the success of the other thermal based plant forms, the challenges of players are more related to the solar thermal patterns.  Higher altitudes mean the sun can be blistering and the cloudy dark hours bitter cold.   The challenge for the living is how to manage the practical day to day needs for living in terms of combustible fuel sources.

The residents of Hotshoe face a unique thermodynamic challenge: they live atop a furnace that provides too much heat for their foundations, yet the high-altitude thin air leaves them shivering as soon as the sun dips behind the volcano’s shoulder.

To survive, they have moved away from traditional wood—which is scarce and burns too quickly—in favor of fuel sources that act as "thermal batteries," storing the mountain's energy for controlled release.

#### The Slag-Brick (Vegetative/mineral compressed fuel bricks)
The primary fuel for domestic heating and cooking is the Slag-Brick, a manufactured fuel source that bridges the two dominant soil types of the valley.
 * Composition: Residents harvest paramo peat, the thick, organic matter accumulated by paramo plants.  Not peat in the real world sense of bogs and scotch, but the parallel is apropos.
 * Paramo peat is mixed or layered with crushed blades of Needle Grass that introduces oxidative metals.
 * When the vegetative/metal material is compressed and dried it will burn with a steady, radiant heat similar to charcoal.
 * Landscape Bond:  Preserving their source of paramo peat requires some concern and care of Hotshoe's residents.
 
#### Brine-Cores (Crystallized Thermal Salts)
- For high-heat crafting, blacksmithing and alchemy residents use Brine-Cores
 - The Cold-Shift: When the sun goes down and the elevation-chill hits, the hamlet doesn't smell like woodsmoke; it smells like the metallic, slightly sulfurous scent of "Recharged Salts" and slow-burning peat.

To provide a high-heat, reactive fuel source that mirrors the properties of magnesium or rapid oxidation, we can introduce Ignis-Dust and Cinder-Slake. These substances connect the residents to the most volatile chemical aspects of the Hotshoe landscape, requiring careful handling to avoid the "Reactive Gas" hazards common in the mines.

#### Ignis-Dust (Magnesium-Rich Volcanic Precipitate)
This is a fine, silvery-white powder harvested from the "Dry Vents" near the eruption zone. It is essentially a naturally occurring form of magnesium and phosphorus, precipitated from high-pressure volcanic gases.
 * The Reaction: When exposed to a spark or a localized heat source, Ignis-Dust undergoes rapid oxidation. It burns with a blinding, white-hot intensity that can reach temperatures high enough to melt obsidian or weld iron-sow plates.
 * Practical Connection: Because it is highly reactive, it cannot be stored in standard wooden crates. Residents store it in Leaded Glass Vials or airtight basalt jars.
 * Landscape Bond: Harvesting Ignis-Dust is the "Gold Rush" of Hotshoe. It only appears after a "Gas-Burp" from the mountain. Scavengers must race to the vents to collect the dust before the high-altitude winds blow it away, connecting their personal wealth to the immediate, unpredictable outbursts of the volcano.

#### Cinder-Slake (Exothermic Mineral Composite)
For a more controlled chemical heat, the residents use Cinder-Slake, which functions similarly to "quicklime" or a self-heating ration pack.
 * The Reaction: This is a mixture of crushed Boiling Brine crystals and "Active Ash" taken from the freshest flows. When a small amount of water (or even damp Ash Yam pulp) is added, a violent exothermic reaction occurs. It does not "burn" with a flame but releases a steady, scalding heat and steam for several hours.
 * Practical Connection: This is the primary fuel for "Field Cooking" and portable heating. Kip likely carries a small pouch of Cinder-Slake to keep his coffee warm or to heat his Wet-Husk Mask during a cold night-shift near the vents.
 * Landscape Bond: This connects the people to the chemistry of the water cycle. Since the reaction requires water—a precious resource in the valley—the use of Cinder-Slake is a tactical choice. It forces residents to calculate the "cost" of heat against their own hydration, reinforcing the scarcity mindset of the high-altitude Paramo.
 
#### Mechanical Risks: The "Flare" Hazard
Using these fuels in the Hotshoe environment carries a specific risk:
 * Ignis-Dust Flare: If a character uses Ignis-Dust near a Jade-Glint vent, the blinding light can cause a "Flash-Blindness" effect (DC 14 Con Save) or, worse, ignite the surrounding Reactive Gas, leading to a 3d6 fire damage explosion.
 * Slake-Scald: If a Cinder-Slake pouch is punctured while it's raining or during a "Mica-Dust Flurry," it can overheat and melt through a standard leather bag, potentially ruining Iron-Sow gear.
